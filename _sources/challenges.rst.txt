Challenges and technical improvements of DRS
============================================

DRS revolutionizes transcriptome analysis by enabling direct sequencing of native RNA, eliminating RT and PCR amplification inherent in conventional approaches :ref:`[9] <ref9>`, :ref:`[34] <ref34>`, :ref:`[39] <ref39>`, :ref:`[118] <ref118>`. This preserves critical biological information (RNA modifications, AS, poly(A) tail lengths) while avoiding amplification biases :ref:`[34] <ref34>`, :ref:`[35] <ref35>`, :ref:`[52] <ref52>`, :ref:`[59] <ref59>`, :ref:`[117] <ref117>`, :ref:`[119] <ref119>`. Despite its great potential in epitranscriptomics and clinical research, DRS's widespread application in high-precision, large-scale studies is hindered by technical limitations :ref:`[39] <ref39>`, :ref:`[59] <ref59>`, :ref:`[108] <ref108>`. Here, we elaborate on major challenges (detection sensitivity, stability, standardization, and applicability to specialized samples) and discuss latest improvement directions, which are crucial for unlocking DRS's potential in basic and translational medicine.

Current technical challenges in DRS
-----------------------------------

**Sample and library preparation limitations**

DRS library preparation requires substantial RNA input (e.g., ONT SQK-RNA004 kit: 300 ng poly(A)-enriched RNA or 1 μg total RNA), posing barriers for low-yield clinical specimens (e.g., plasma/exosome RNA: 10-35 ng from 9 mL plasma) :ref:`[59] <ref59>`. Although sample multiplexing is explored to meet input thresholds, lack of commercialized, standardized solutions from ONT limits clinical reproducibility and scalability.

In terms of single-cell and spatial transcriptomics applications, these are severely constrained by ultra-low per-cell RNA (10-30 pg), far below kit requirements. RNA loss, degradation, nonspecific adsorption, and contamination (even trace exogenous RNA) during isolation, extraction, and library construction reduce experimental success and data reliability.

Additionally, most commercial DRS kits rely on poly(A) enrichment to capture mRNA, neglecting abundant non-poly(A) RNAs (miRNAs, lncRNAs, circRNAs, degraded transcripts) in single cells :ref:`[108] <ref108>`, :ref:`[119] <ref119>`—molecules critical for regulating cellular heterogeneity. While laboratory-based non-poly(A) enrichment methods exist, their lack of commercialization restricts transcriptome comprehensiveness and highlights the need for inclusive capture strategies.

**Technical detection limitations**

One major issue in technical detection is the inefficient detection of short RNAs: DRS struggles to detect short RNAs shorter than 100-150 nt :ref:`[59] <ref59>`, which are critical for many biological processes including gene regulation and immune response. While recent updates to the MinKNOW software have improved detection sensitivity for RNA molecules longer than 50 nt, biologically relevant short RNAs, such as 22-45 nt cell-free RNAs in liquid biopsies that serve as potential disease biomarkers, remain largely undetectable. Currently, laboratory-developed protocols for small RNA DRS are not widely adopted due to their complexity and poor reproducibility, leading to the misclassification of short RNAs as adapter sequences and resulting in significant gaps in small RNA transcriptomic profiling.

Another limitation concerns reduced resolution at RNA 5′ termini. DRS proceeds in the 3′ to 5′ direction, therefore the 5′ end of the molecule is not regulated by the motor protein during the final stage of translocation. As a result, approximately the last 15 nt traverse the nanopore at an accelerated rate, which compromises the stability and fidelity of ionic current measurements and ultimately limits accurate characterization of 5′ terminal sequences :ref:`[35] <ref35>`, :ref:`[59] <ref59>`, :ref:`[108] <ref108>`, :ref:`[191] <ref191>`. This leads to loss or poor resolution of 5′ regulatory elements (cap structure, promoter-proximal sequences, UTRs), hindering transcript isoform characterization and gene regulation analysis.

Regarding sequencing throughput, DRS avoids amplification biases by sequencing native RNA but has lower throughput than amplification-based platforms (Illumina/PacBio). Despite enzyme engineering advances (high-speed helicases ~150 nt/s, stable nanopores) improving output by an order of magnitude, sequencing depth/yield remains limiting for high-coverage large-scale studies :ref:`[39] <ref39>`, :ref:`[59] <ref59>`, :ref:`[108] <ref108>`, :ref:`[191] <ref191>`.

Nanopore blockage and signal drift also compromise sequencing performance. Blockage events caused by sample contaminants, persistent RNA secondary structures, or improper enzyme interactions can result in irreversible pore inactivation. During prolonged sequencing runs, variations in temperature and ionic strength progressively shift the baseline ionic current, leading to signal drift that diminishes data stability and interpretability. Together, these effects reduce base calling consistency and undermine the accuracy and reproducibility of sequencing results :ref:`[39] <ref39>`, :ref:`[640] <ref640>`, :ref:`[685] <ref685>`.

**Technical consistency and bioinformatics gaps**

In terms of technical reproducibility, technical replicate consistency is compromised by MinKNOW base-calling algorithm variations (dependent on software version/parameters), leading to different read subsets from identical raw data :ref:`[40] <ref40>`, :ref:`[41] <ref41>`, :ref:`[639] <ref639>` and introducing unwanted variability. DRS kit shelf stability is unstandardized: unlike ONT DNA "Q Series" (3-year stability), DRS reagents have inter-batch enzyme performance variability, affecting cross-experiment data comparability :ref:`[639] <ref639>`, :ref:`[640] <ref640>`. 

Another aspect affecting data reliability is the uneven base-calling accuracy. DRS base-calling accuracy is lower than short-read sequencing and uneven along transcripts, with signal degradation at termini (especially 5′ end) :ref:`[119] <ref119>`, :ref:`[640] <ref640>`, complicating transcript boundary mapping and isoform analysis.

Regarding standardization gaps, DRS lacks standardized commercial RNA reference materials with defined modification profiles; ERCC/SIRV spike-ins (used in traditional RNA-seq) are incompatible with epitranscriptomic analysis :ref:`[115] <ref115>`, :ref:`[118] <ref118>`. In-house in vitro transcribed RNAs (used as controls) vary in quality/modifications, undermining cross-study reproducibility.

Additionally, bioinformatics pipeline instability and modification detection challenges further limit DRS application: rapid advances in DRS chemistry/base-calling render dedicated bioinformatics tools obsolete (early software fails to support new chemistries) :ref:`[118] <ref118>`, disrupting data processing continuity. DRS modification calling (relying on subtle ionic current deviations) is sensitive to technical noise, increasing false positives :ref:`[162] <ref162>`, :ref:`[280] <ref280>`; accurate modification stoichiometry quantification at specific sites also remains challenging, limiting epitranscriptomic profiling reliability.

To address the aforementioned technical challenges limiting nanopore DRS in high-precision epitranscriptomic research and clinical translation, extensive technological innovation and optimization have been pursued recently. Below, we systematically summarize the latest advances and key improvement directions of nanopore DRS, focusing on hardware upgrading, algorithmic optimization, standardization establishment, and application expansion to overcome current technical bottlenecks. 

Technological improvement directions of DRS
-------------------------------------------

Starting with hardware innovation, this field relies on synergistic evolution of membrane materials and molecular motors. While biological nanopores are DRS gold standards, their resolution/stability limitations drive AI-augmented enzyme engineering (e.g., Huimin Zhao et al.'s autonomous AI platforms enabling high-throughput design of high-fidelity, processive enzymes) :ref:`[686] <ref686>`. Solid-state/hybrid pore architectures (mechanical robustness, complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor integration potential) aim for superior SNR and precise molecular translocation contro :ref:`[687] <ref687>`; 2D materials (graphene, MoS2) offer atomic-scale spatial resolution, potentially enabling enzyme-free base/modification discrimination :ref:`[687] <ref687>`.

Notably, high-confidence single-molecule protein sequencing via DRS requires overcoming multi-dimensional bottlenecks (stochastic motor-pore assembly, spatio-temporal noise compression, peptide-driven ionic current deciphering); scarce high-quality ground-truth datasets hinder supervised learning model robustness in proteomic deconvolution :ref:`[688] <ref688>`.

In terms of algorithms, DRS data analysis is shifting from task-specific algorithms to foundation model-driven intelligence, with genomic language models (gLMs) capturing long-range dependencies to improve base-calling/modification prediction. While m6A detection is mature, epitranscriptomic "dark matter" (Ψ, m5C, A-to-I) requires new tools optimized for specificity/quantification :ref:`[689] <ref689>`, :ref:`[690] <ref690>`. Tailored architectures (mirroring nanopore signal biophysics) outperform generalized massive models :ref:`[689] <ref689>`, :ref:`[690] <ref690>`; retrained models (validated via orthogonal data) ensure non-canonical modification call fidelity.

Certainly, DRS maturation requires rigorous standardization and real-time clinical workflows: high-fidelity RNA reference materials, unified benchmarking frameworks, and community-driven data standards are needed for clinical translation :ref:`[688] <ref688>`. Real-time analysis integrated with adaptive sampling enables dynamic target enrichment and instant clinical decision-making :ref:`[28] <ref28>`; automated "sample-to-report" pipelines and regulatory validation will expand DRS from infectious disease surveillance to standardized precision medicine.

In summary, DRS is indispensable in epitranscriptomics, offering preprocessing-free, single-molecule, long-read multi-modification analysis. Despite prominent challenges (high input, poor short RNA detection, uneven accuracy, insufficient standardization, bioinformatics gaps), continuous hardware/algorithmic advances and standardization efforts will gradually address these limitations. DRS will drive epitranscriptomics toward higher precision, broader coverage, and greater accessibility, supporting RNA modification function research and development of modification-based diagnostic/therapeutic tools, ultimately paving the way for precision epitranscriptomics and revolutionizing disease diagnosis/treatment.
