Nanopore direct RNA sequencing: fundamental principles and developmental trajectory
===================================================================================

DRS employs nanopore sequencing technology to perform single-molecule sequencing of native RNA molecules :ref:`[34] <ref34>`. Unlike traditional approaches, this method does not require RT of RNA into cDNA, thereby effectively avoiding biases that may be introduced during RT and PCR amplification :ref:`[35] <ref35>`. Its fundamental principle is that, under an applied electric field, single-stranded RNA molecules pass through a biological nanopore embedded in a membrane. Because RNA molecules carry a negative charge, a motor protein drives the RNA into the nanopore at a controlled speed under voltage. Typically, the motor protein attaches to the poly(A) tail at the 3′ end of mRNA via an adapter sequence, enabling the RNA to translocate through the pore in a 3′-to-5′ direction. When a short segment of the RNA strand (approximately 5 nt) occupies the narrow constriction of the nanopore, it impedes ionic current flow and produces characteristic changes in the electrical signal :ref:`[36] <ref36>`, :ref:`[37] <ref37>`. Different nucleotide sequence combinations generate current changes of varying magnitudes, allowing the electrical signals to be regarded as “fingerprints” of the corresponding k-mer sequences :ref:`[9] <ref9>`, :ref:`[35] <ref35>`, :ref:`[38] <ref38>`, :ref:`[39] <ref39>`. The sequencing device records these current signals in real time and interprets them into nucleotide sequences using deep learning algorithms, a process known as basecalling :ref:`[38] <ref38>`, :ref:`[40] <ref40>`. High-accuracy models developed by ONT utilize neural networks to analyze current waveforms, enabling real-time reading of RNA sequences :ref:`[41] <ref41>`, :ref:`[42] <ref42>`.

While DRS has only recently matured into a robust platform, the conceptual framework for this technology was established decades prior to its commercial realization. In 1989, the American scientist David Deamer first proposed the idea of driving single-stranded DNA or RNA molecules through nanoscale pores to read their sequence information :ref:`[43] <ref43>`. In the mid-1990s, key experimental advances were achieved. Kasianowicz and colleagues observed ionic current blockade events when single-stranded nucleic acids passed through an α-hemolysin biological pore and this discovery laid the foundation for subsequent single-molecule sequencing :ref:`[43] <ref43>`. By the late 1990s, “nanopore sequencing” gradually emerged and was adopted as a formal term and technological approach. During the same period, Hagan Bayley and colleagues conducted related technical research. They developed a method to detect molecular characteristics by measuring stochastic fluctuations in nanopore ionic currents, known as stochastic sensing :ref:`[44] <ref44>`, :ref:`[45] <ref45>`. They also recognized that this approach could be applied to DNA sequencing. The Staphylococcus aureus membrane channel protein α-hemolysin has an inner diameter of approximately 1.4-2.4 nm :ref:`[43] <ref43>`, :ref:`[46] <ref46>`. It was the first nanopore proven to allow the passage of RNA and DNA homopolymers while producing identifiable ionic current blockade signals :ref:`[43] <ref43>`, :ref:`[47] <ref47>`, :ref:`[48] <ref48>`.

The founding of ONT by Hagan Bayley and Gordon Sanghera in 2005 greatly accelerated the translation of these fundamental concepts into a practical sequencing platform and the transition of nanopore sequencing from a conceptual stage to real-world application :ref:`[43] <ref43>`. In the decade following ONT’s establishment, the company’s research team continuously worked on optimizing biological nanopore materials and sequencing enzymes :ref:`[39] <ref39>`. For example, they engineered the protein CsgG to develop the R9 series of nanopores and upgraded sequencing enzymes, gradually enabling high-throughput DNA sequencing :ref:`[49] <ref49>`, :ref:`[50] <ref50>`.

This innovation broke the dependence of traditional sequencing on laboratory settings and enabled long-read DNA sequencing to be performed outside conventional laboratories :ref:`[43] <ref43>`. In December of 2016, ONT provided the first DRS reagent kits (code: SQK-RNA001) to a small group of researchers for testing (https://nanoporetech.com/news/news-direct-rna-sequencing-minion-new-paper-preprint). In April 2017, ONT further expanded access by officially making RNA direct sequencing kits available to its user community(https://nanoporetech.com/news/news-direct-rna-sequencing-nanopore-opens-more-users), and preliminary experimental results of direct RNA sequencing were presented at the London Calling conference in May of the same year (https://nanoporetech.com/news/news-london-calling-2017-day-2-updates). In 2018, the ONT research team published the core findings on DRS in Nature Methods :ref:`[9] <ref9>`. The reported DRS method did not require RT or amplification steps. It successfully determined the full-length sequences of yeast mRNA transcripts. The study also experimentally demonstrated the feasibility of directly identifying RNA base modifications through electrical signal analysis :ref:`[9] <ref9>`.

Since the introduction of the first-generation RNA001 kit, DRS has continued to evolve, achieving major upgrades at several key stages :ref:`[51] <ref51>` (Figure 2B). Studies based on this kit demonstrated the value of DRS in complex transcriptome analysis, a 2019 study of the human polyadenylated transcriptome used DRS to simultaneously analyze transcript diversity, poly(A) tail length distribution, and RNA modifications :ref:`[35] <ref35>`. In 2020, a plant transcriptome study showed that DRS could reveal splicing isoforms, alternative polyadenylation (APA) site selection, and m6A modification patterns in A. thaliana mRNA :ref:`[52] <ref52>`. Building upon the RNA001 kit, ONT released an improved RNA sequencing kit, SQK-RNA002, in 2019. This updated version substantially improved both data yield and stability, and became the mainstream DRS solution between 2019 and 2023 :ref:`[53] <ref53>`. In 2024, ONT launched the fourth-generation direct RNA sequencing kit, SQK-RNA004, representing a major technological upgrade :ref:`[54] <ref54>` (Figure 2C). This kit employs nanopore proteins specifically optimized for RNA sequencing.

Crucially, current DRS enables multidimensional data acquisition beyond simple sequence determination, with its prominent advantage residing in the direct reading of native RNA molecules that facilitates the acquisition of full-length, strand-specific transcript sequences while preserving and accurately detecting RNA base modifications :ref:`[9] <ref9>`. Due to this approach does not require cDNA conversion, chemical modifications on RNA, such as m6A, Ψ, m5C, and inosine, preserve their subtle influences on ionic current during sequencing and can therefore be effectively identified and analyzed :ref:`[34] <ref34>`, :ref:`[55] <ref55>`, :ref:`[56] <ref56>`, :ref:`[57] <ref57>` (Figure 2D). The latest DRS basecalling algorithms incorporate dedicated electrical signal models that enable the simultaneous direct detection of multiple RNA modifications during sequencing and the precise annotation of their positions :ref:`[58] <ref58>`. Because the 3′ poly(A) tail of mRNA consists of repeated identical nucleotides, its corresponding segment produces a low-variance signal in the sequencing current. By analyzing this signal and correcting for the translocation speed of the molecule through the nanopore, the poly(A) tail length of each transcript can be accurately estimated :ref:`[35] <ref35>`. Dedicated software tools, such as nanopolish-polya, have been developed for this purpose. These tools segment the raw electrical signal into poly(A) regions and measure their dwell time. And then the number of adenosines in the tail can be inferred from these measurements :ref:`[35] <ref35>`, :ref:`[52] <ref52>`. In summary, nanopore DRS enables long-read, amplification-free RNA sequencing through the interpretation of electrical current signals. This technology not only provides sequence information but also simultaneously captures multiple layers of transcriptomic features, including splicing variation, transcript structure, base modifications, and poly(A) tail length :ref:`[52] <ref52>`, :ref:`[59] <ref59>`.

ONT also released a new generation of open-source basecalling software, Dorado (https://nanoporetech.com/platform/accuracy), which employs improved deep-learning basecalling models that enhance accuracy and speed. In a head-to-head comparison, RNA004 data basecalled with Dorado achieved a median alignment identity of 98.67% (reads ≥ 200 aligned bases), In a direct comparison, RNA004 reads basecalled with Dorado showed a median alignment identity of 98.67% for reads with at least 200 aligned bases, while RNA002 reads basecalled with Guppy reached 90.65%, highlighting the higher single-read accuracy obtained when using RNA004 together with Dorado :ref:`[53] <ref53>`. Deep learning models of Dorado also support de novo detection of multiple RNA modifications, such as 2′‑O‑methylation (Nm), Ψ, m6A, m5C, and inosine, within a single DRS run. In validation experiments with RNA004 data, Dorado’s Ψ and m6A models achieved high performance, with accuracies and F1-scores generally in the mid-90 % range or higher :ref:`[53] <ref53>`, :ref:`[60] <ref60>`. These improvements mark the entry of nanopore DRS into a high-accuracy era, enabling more reliable characterization of transcript sequences and epitranscriptomic features :ref:`[61] <ref61>`. From proof-of-concept to the implementation of RNA001 and RNA002, and now to the maturation of RNA004 combined with Dorado, each technological iteration represents a step toward higher accuracy and broader application of DRS :ref:`[53] <ref53>`. These advances provide powerful tools for comprehensive analysis of transcriptional regulation. With continued optimization of algorithms and sequencing chemistries, DRS is expected to play an increasingly important role in transcriptome research, RNA modification mapping, and RNA-based therapeutic development :ref:`[53] <ref53>`, :ref:`[62] <ref62>`.
