Outlook: toward integrative, haplotype-aware RNA biology

From feature catalogues to multilayer regulatory logic

DRS has rapidly moved beyond proof-of-concept demonstrations toward integrative discovery, fulfilling a key early promise of the technology: the ability to read transcript structure, poly(A) properties, and multiple RNA modification signals simultaneously on native RNA molecules [51], [119]. This single-assay, multi-layer readout substantially reduces experimental fragmentation compared with running parallel assays for isoforms, 3′ ends, and epitranscriptomics, and has begun to reposition DRS from a niche alternative to RNA-seq into a regulatory scaffold for plant transcriptomics. A practical application of this transition is timely as the output of DRS is unusually model-ready. Each read naturally yields structured attributes, isoform identity, cleavage site choice, tail length estimates, and modification probabilities, that can be assembled into transcript- or gene-level knowledge graphs. Such graphs provide a substrate for representation learning and are increasingly compatible with large language model (LLM)-assisted biological reasoning, as already explored in crop-focused knowledge graph frameworks [38], [691]. Standardized DRS-derived feature sets could therefore serve as a bridge between experimental transcriptomics and AI-enabled hypothesis generation (Figure 10A).

From bulk tissues toward cell types and spatial contexts

Bulk DRS studies consistently imply that RNA regulation is highly context-specific, particularly for isoform switching, APA remodeling, and RNA modification dynamics. However, most current mechanistic inference still relies on tissue averages. Early long-read single-nucleus approaches in plants, although based on cDNA rather than native RNA, have already shown that AS and APA patterns differ sharply across cell populations, underscoring the value of long reads for resolving regulatory heterogeneity [692]. True single-cell DRS remains constrained by input requirements, signal noise, and cost, particularly when modification inference is included [119], [640]. In the near term, a more realistic path is therefore hybrid strategies: use short-read single-cell or single-nucleus data to define cell states and candidate regulatory programs, then apply bulk or targeted DRS to enriched tissues to read out multilayer RNA features with high confidence. Even without single-cell DRS, integrating bulk DRS with spatial transcriptomics or cell-type marker frameworks can localize regulatory programs to anatomical contexts.

Accessing to a comprehensive repository of RNA modifications

Epitranscriptomics has so far been dominated by m6A and m5C, yet accumulating evidence indicates that additional modifications, such as hm5C and ac4C, contribute to development, stress responses, and long-distance RNA transport [693], [694]. DRS is uniquely attractive here because it can, in principle, detect multiple modification signatures on the same native RNA molecules and relate them directly to isoform usage, APA decisions, and stability proxies. A key future direction is to move from single-accession snapshots toward pan-epitranscriptomes, mirroring the conceptual shift introduced by pangenomes [202]. Population-scale DRS could distinguish conserved modification sites that form regulatory backbones from variable, genotype- or environment-specific marks that tune phenotypes (Figure 10C). Methodologically, this expansion depends on robust and transferable modification callers. Encouragingly, recent benchmarking and transfer-learning approaches suggest that nanopore signal perturbations induced by RNA modifications are sufficiently conserved to allow cross-species model adaptation, providing unprecedented chance to construct a comprehensive repository of RNA modifications across species [174], [176], [179].

Plant genome complexity as an advantage: TEs, haplotypes, and evolution

Plants are enriched for the two genome features that most challenge transcriptomics, TEs and polyploidy, but these same features make DRS particularly powerful. Long-read native RNA sequencing can directly resolve TE-derived isoforms, chimeric gene-TE transcripts, and alternative termination within repetitive contexts that confound short reads [275], [352]. These studies reveal TE-derived transcripts are not annotation artifacts but regulatory entities shaped by RNAPII elongation, epigenetic state, and alternative poly(A) signal usage. In polyploid crops and diverse germplasm panels, DRS can also function as an allele- and haplotype-aware readout, enabling ASE and, potentially, allele-specific RNA modification and APA analyses. This capability opens the door to evolutionary transcriptomics that treats TE-derived isoforms and allele-specific RNA regulation not as analytical noise, but as substrates for adaptation (Figure 10D). Importantly, because polyploidization is pervasive across the plant kingdom, particularly in wild and non-model species [695], DRS is uniquely positioned to unlock transcriptomic regulation in complex genomes where traditional short-read approaches are most limited.

Clinical and Translational Perspectives: Toward RNA-Informed Precision Medicine

DRS offers a powerful framework for future clinical diagnostics by enabling direct, amplification-free profiling of full-length native RNAs together with their modification states (Figure 10E). Its translational impact, however, depends on overcoming key challenges, including analytical sensitivity, clinical-grade accuracy, and standardized workflows compatible with regulatory requirements [189], [640]. As these barriers are progressively addressed, DRS has the potential to shift medical diagnostics from static, gene-centric measurements toward dynamic, RNA-centered molecular profiling [696], [697].

In oncology and liquid biopsy applications, DRS can resolve oncogenic fusion transcripts, tumor-specific splice isoforms, and dynamic RNA modification landscapes from trace amounts of circulating RNA, features largely inaccessible to conventional DNA-based or short-read approaches [698], [699]. In neurological and infectious diseases, DRS enables direct interrogation of pathogen and host transcriptomes, capturing RNA processing and modification dynamics that may inform early diagnosis, disease monitoring, and therapeutic decision-making [700], [701]. Beyond diagnostics, DRS is emerging as a promising platform for quality control of RNA therapeutics, allowing single-molecule assessment of sequence integrity, poly(A) tail length, and nucleotide modifications in mRNA vaccines and RNA drugs. Together, these advances position DRS to support a transition toward continuous, RNA-informed precision medicine.

Toward routine deployment: standardization and multi-omics integration

Despite its transformative potential, the widespread adoption of DRS remains constrained by cost, signal noise, and analytical complexity, particularly for modification inference [51]. A realistic path forward is standardization by task: defining best-practice workflows for transcript structure and APA analysis, poly(A) tail profiling, and RNA modification calling, each with explicit quality-control metrics and computational expectations (Figure 10F). Community benchmarks and shared reference datasets will be essential for ensuring reproducibility across laboratories and application domains. In parallel, the greatest impact of DRS is likely to arise from deep integration with complementary omics rather than stand-alone deployment. Positioning DRS as the native-RNA layer alongside srRNA-seq, high-quality genome assemblies, chromatin accessibility assays, ribosome profiling, proteomics, and metabolomics has already revealed why transcript abundance alone fails to predict protein output and how post-transcriptional regulation reshapes biological responses [346], [352], [702]. In biomedical contexts, such integration underpins emerging applications in liquid biopsy, infectious disease surveillance, and RNA therapeutic quality control, where preserving the native linkage between sequence, structure, and modification is critical for accurate interpretation. Realizing the full potential of DRS will require coordinated advances in protocol optimization, computational efficiency, and regulatory-grade validation. As these barriers are addressed, DRS is poised to evolve from a powerful research technology into a foundational platform for mapping RNA regulation in its full molecular complexity.
